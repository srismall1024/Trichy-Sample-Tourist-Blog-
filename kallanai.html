Kallanai Dam

One of the oldest water-regulator structures in the world and the oldest in India still in operation is Kallanai, also called the Grand Anicut.

Constructed by the Chola king Karikalan between 100 and 150 CE across the Kaveri River in the modern-day Thanjavur district, roughly 15 km from Tiruchirappalli, it was intended to improve irrigation by rerouting water to the fertile delta districts.

The dam is 329 meters long, 20 meters wide, and 5.4 meters high, and it was built from unhewn stone. Nearly 69,000 acres were irrigated in antiquity thanks to its brilliant engineering, and by the 20th century, that number had grown to almost one million acres.

Renovated by the British in the 19th century, it saw improvements like added height and undersluices to prevent silt buildup. Sir Arthur Cotton later constructed the Lower Anaicut, inspired by Kallanai.

Today, with the Kaveri branching into Kollidam, Vennaru, and Puthu Aru before reaching the Bay of Bengal, the Grand Anicut remains a marvel of engineering and a cultural landmark, admired for its timeless practicality. Farmers have long called for honoring Karikalan Chola for his visionary contribution.
